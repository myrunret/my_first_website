How small are microplastics?

You might picture plastic pollution as fish nets, bottles and bags floating at the surface of the ocean. However, plastic pollution can be also invisible to us.

Plastic bottle
~20 cm

A single plastic bottle discarded today will outlive everyone alive on Earth. As the time passes, it doesn't decompose completely, but rather breaks into millions of invisible fragments, contaminating global marine ecosystems.

Macroplastic
> 5 mm

It is a common misconception that plastic is solely an environmental problem. Under relentless UV light and friction, every piece of plastic slowly degrades, shedding particles that sink, disperse, and enter the food chain invisibly, carrying other chemicals into our bodies.

Microplastic
5 mm — 1 µm

Microplastics are now found in every corner of the globe, from the deepest ocean trenches to Arctic ice. They are ingested by marine life and have been detected in human blood and tissues.

Nanoplastic
< 1 µm

The smallest particles, nanoplastics, are still being studied but may penetrate cells and cross biological barriers. Their health impacts remain an urgent area of research.

It's Already Inside You

The results of cellular and animal experiments have shown that microplastics can affect various systems in the human body, including the digestive, respiratory, endocrine, reproductive, and immune systems.

Consequences

Microplastics are not an instant poison. The concern is slower: particles and plastic chemicals may add stress to tissues, blood vessels, and developing bodies over time.

77% of donors had plastic particles in blood in Leslie et al. 2022
90% of donors had particles detected in Leonard et al. 2024
4.5x higher risk of heart attack, stroke, or death when artery plaques contained plastics
Human biomonitoring Leslie 2022; Leonard 2024

How much gets into blood?

Leslie et al. found particles in 77% of healthy volunteers, averaging 1.6 µg/mL. Leonard et al. later found 24 polymer types in blood, detected in up to 90% of donors.

The useful update is precision: instead of only estimating ingestion, newer studies directly measure polymers circulating in human blood.

Sources: Leslie 2022, Leonard 2024, Marfella 2024, Ragusa 2021, Zhou 2025.
